About

As recently as 30 years ago, in the 1990s, studies showed that most people preferred getting information from others rather than information retrieval systems. But, of course, most people also used human travel agents to book their travel then too. During the last decade, however, the relentless optimization of information retrieval systems has increased their effectiveness such that they are now the standard for finding information online. Today, the US search market alone is over 100 billion dollars. Information Retrieval is the activity of obtaining material –usually documents– of an unstructured nature –particularly text– which satisfies an information need from within extensive collections stored on computers. Information retrieval methods are widely used in many systems, yet, these systems require the IR method to be fast enough to be used in a live environment. However, highly performant models are not very efficient in a production setting.

Our goal is to create a model that increases effectiveness while decreasing latency through sparse representations. We will measure effectiveness with Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR)@k and Recall@k and efficiency gains through a decrease in FLOPs.

Datasets & Models

Splade is a neural retrieval model which learns query/document sparse expansion via the BERT MLM head and sparse regularization. Splade is an acronym for SParse Lexical AnD Expansion Model for First Stage Ranking. Splade attempts to solve the longstanding vocabulary mismatch problem in information retrieval–wherein documents that do not explicitly contain an exact match of the search terms are not returned–through query expansion. Query expansions enhance queries to obtain hits on more passages/documents.

ColBERT is a ranking model that adapts BERT for efficient retrieval. The ColBERT architecture follows the bi-encoder paradigm where it independently encodes the query and the document using a shared BERT model and then calculates their similarity. Similarity is determined by Late Interaction, which is determined by summing each query embeddings’ maximum similarity score against all document embedding vectors. This process allows ColBERT to pre-compute document representations offline, which speeds up query processing, while retaining momo-encoder’s fine-graining word interactions which is typically lost in bi-encoders.

MSMARCO: MS MARCO is a collection of datasets for deep learning related to Search, with all questions coming from anonymized user queries from Bing. The context passages were extracted from web documents returned in the search results and summarized by humans. This dataset is a collection of 8.8 million passages. The small training data consists of 40M triples, and the full training data consists of 400M triples. We evaluate our model using the official development set contains 101k queries. 55k of those 101k queries are graded to determine performance as reported in this paper.

ColBERT Results

For ColBERT, we explored the effects of pruning on the performance of information retrieval models. There are two types of pruning: structured and unstructured. Structured pruning involves removing entire layers from the model, while unstructured pruning removes individual connections within a layer.

>

Structured pruning reduces the number of parameters in the model, leading to a smaller model size and potentially improved performance. However, it does not introduce sparsity into the model since no weights are actually pruned. Instead, structured pruning is used to optimize the architecture of the model.

>

On the other hand, unstructured pruning removes the least salient connections within a layer, introducing sparsity into the model. The saliency of a connection is typically determined using the magnitude of the weight as a proxy. After pruning, the model is usually retrained to fine-tune the remaining connections. As our goal is to increase the sparsity of the model, we focus on unstructured pruning. We show that even without retraining, pruning can improve performance.

>

We used ColBERT to explore the effects of global unstructured L1 pruning on different modules at various pruning percentages. Our experiment setup uses PyTorch and the HuggingFace Transformers library, and we trained ColBERT using default settings with a batch size of 32 and a maximum query length of 32 and document length of 220. We trained the initial model to 500k steps on an NVIDIA 3090, and all other models were trained to 80k steps on a single NVIDIA T4 Tensor Core GPU.

>

We applied global unstructured L1 pruning to four different modules of ColBERT: the Feed Forward, Pooler, and final linear layers, as well as the Self-Attention and Attention modules. Our results show two main groupings in terms of performance. The first grouping, which includes the pruning of the Attention and Self-Attention modules, shows a stable increase in performance up to a certain pruning percentage, after which it begins to steadily decrease. The second grouping, which includes the pruning of the other modules, shows slight gains with low levels of pruning, but this increase quickly degrades as the pruning percentage increases, leading to a sharp decrease in performance from 45% to 60%.

>

These results demonstrate the importance of considering the distribution of weight magnitudes when applying global pruning. We found that the dense layers inside the BERT Pooler and final linear layers had a magnitude that was 10 times less than the other layers, which makes using weight magnitude as a proxy for determining the saliency of connections unreliable. In future work, we plan to observe the effects of pruning all dense layers inside the BERT Encoder and only the BERT Pooler and final linear layer, as well as explore non-global unstructured L1 pruning methods and more dynamic forms of pruning.

>

Additionally, we conducted an extension of our experiment, applying global unstructured L1 pruning to the Attention module at different pruning percentages. We observed that across all experiments, introducing even a small amount of pruning into ColBERT resulted in gains in sparsity and performance. In another extension of the experiment, we added L1 regularization to the loss function with various regularization parameters. We found that while weights were lowered, no values were dropped to 0, and the only notable gains were seen with a regularization parameter of 5e-6. Overall, our results show that adding L1 regularization does not significantly increase the sparsity of ColBERT.
